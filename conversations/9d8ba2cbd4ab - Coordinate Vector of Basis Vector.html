Determine whether the following statement is true or false, and explain your answer with equations.


> The B-coordinate vector of a vector in $B$ is a standard vector.

The statement is true.

Explanation

Let B={b1​,b2​,…,bn​} be a basis for a vector space V. A "vector in B" refers to one of the basis vectors themselves. Let's choose an arbitrary basis vector bj​ from the set B, where j is an integer such that 1≤j≤n.

We want to determine the B-coordinate vector of bj​, denoted as [bj​]B​. By definition, the B-coordinate vector [bj​]B​ is a column vector ​c1​c2​⋮cn​​​ such that:

bj​=c1​b1​+c2​b2​+⋯+cj​bj​+⋯+cn​bn​

To satisfy this equation, we can express bj​ as a linear combination of the basis vectors in B. We are looking for the specific coefficients c1​,c2​,…,cn​. We can write bj​ as:

bj​=0⋅b1​+0⋅b2​+⋯+1⋅bj​+⋯+0⋅bn​

Comparing this equation with the definition of the coordinate vector, we can see that the coefficients are: ci​=0 for i=j cj​=1

Therefore, the B-coordinate vector of bj​ is:

[bj​]B​=​0⋮010⋮0​​

where the '1' is in the j-th position.

This resulting vector is a standard vector (also known as a standard basis vector or a canonical basis vector). For an n-dimensional space, the j-th standard vector ej​ is a vector with a 1 in the j-th component and 0s in all other components.

Thus, the B-coordinate vector of any vector bj​ in the basis B is the j-th standard vector ej​.
